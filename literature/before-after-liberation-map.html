One Story, Many Geographies

Before and After Liberation Map

This map separates the story's geography into three layers: places Hyŏn directly inhabits, places the text strongly implies but does not name precisely, and places that matter because Hyŏn thinks through them. That distinction matters here. 해방전후 is not only a Seoul story or a liberation story. It is also a rural retreat story, a border story, and a world-historical argument carried by place references.

Map Logic
Direct
Named settings or institutions directly traversed in the plot.
Inferred
Settings the story locates strongly enough to map approximately. Here the best-supported rural setting is former Anhyŏp (안협), not just a generic mountain town.
Allusive
External places invoked to frame liberation, loyalty, retreat, and world history.

Story Geography

The page treats precision honestly. Exact locations are marked as such. Approximate settings are labeled inferred. For the rural retreat, the strongest identification is former Anhyŏp (안협): Yi Taejun is documented as having withdrawn there in 1943, and the story's clues match the upper Imjin basin better than Ch'ŏlwŏn town itself. Long-distance travel is shown through place markers rather than a single straight line, because the story does not specify an exact road.

Important But Unmappable

Two of the story's most important geographies are intentionally not plot points on a real map. They are moral fantasies used to test whether retreat is still possible in the twentieth century.

Yao-and-Shun village

Hyŏn asks where, under a Japanese sky, one could still find the age of sage-kings. This is not a destination. It is a measure of impossibility.

Peach Blossom Spring

The story invokes the old utopian valley only to deny its availability. The allusion matters because it marks the death of classical withdrawal as a serious option.
