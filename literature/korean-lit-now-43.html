About the Issue

Korean Literature Now is a bilingual literary journal published by the Literature Translation Institute of Korea (LTI Korea, 한국문학번역원) to introduce Korean literature to international readers. Volume 43 (Spring 2019) was a landmark queer special issue — one of the first mainstream Korean literary platforms to dedicate an entire issue to LGBT voices, at a moment when queer visibility in Korean society was rapidly increasing but also provoking significant backlash.

The issue pairs critical framing (Yang Gyeong-eon's essay) with fiction and poetry, giving readers both a theoretical lens and primary literary examples. Reading them together is the point: the essay teaches you how to see what the stories are doing.

Queer Literature in South Korea: Context

South Korea's LGBT community has gained significant visibility since the early 2000s — the Seoul Queer Culture Festival (서울퀴어문화축제), founded in 2000, has grown into one of Asia's largest Pride events, though it has also faced organized opposition from conservative Christian groups. Legally, same-sex relationships are neither criminalized nor officially recognized.

Korean queer literature has a longer underground history, but its mainstream visibility accelerated after the early 2010s. Writers like Choi Eun-yong and Choi Jinyoung brought queer themes into acclaimed literary fiction that reached wide audiences — not niche publications. This is the literary generation this issue represents.

Yang Gyeong-eon's critical essay provides the theoretical framing: what makes literature "queer"? Is it the identity of the author, the content, the form, or the relationship to normative structures? Her argument that queer literature is a "life source" positions it as generative rather than merely marginal.

Historical Context: The Politics of Visibility

The 2019 issue came out during a period of intensifying culture-war conflict over LGBT rights in Korea. Conservative Protestant Christian organizations — a powerful social bloc — had mobilized aggressively against Pride events, anti-discrimination legislation, and queer visibility in culture. Several attempts to pass a comprehensive anti-discrimination law (차별금지법) that would protect LGBT people have stalled in the National Assembly for over a decade.

Placing this anthology in a course on Korean history through literature is a statement: queer experience is not marginal to Korean history — it is Korean history, including the history of who gets to be visible and who does not.

Key Concepts

Seoul Queer Culture Festival (서울퀴어문화축제)
Annual Pride event founded 2000, now one of Asia's largest, held in Seoul City Hall Plaza — and a site of sustained counter-protest by conservative Christian groups.
Anti-Discrimination Law (차별금지법)
A comprehensive bill that would prohibit discrimination based on sexual orientation and gender identity, among other categories. Proposed repeatedly in the National Assembly since 2007; blocked each time by conservative opposition.
LTI Korea (한국문학번역원)
Literature Translation Institute of Korea — the state body that funds and promotes translation of Korean literature abroad. Its decision to publish a queer special issue was itself a political act.

Discussion Questions

1. Yang Gyeong-eon calls queer literature the "life source" of Korean literature's present. What does she mean? Do you find the argument convincing?
2. How do the fictional works in this issue represent queer experience — as exceptional or ordinary? What is the effect of each approach?
3. Compare the presence of queer themes in this anthology with the near-total absence of such themes in the earlier texts on the syllabus. What does this contrast reveal about Korean literary history?
4. The fact that LTI Korea — a government body — published this issue is significant. What does state support for queer literature mean in a country where anti-discrimination protections for LGBT people remain blocked?
5. How does week 8 pair with Spirit's Homecoming (the comfort women film) on the film side? What do the two texts share in terms of their engagement with gender, the body, and state power?
