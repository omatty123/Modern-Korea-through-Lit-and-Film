The Expert Record

Victor Cha: Prediction vs. Reality

Cha is Korea Chair, CSIS. Former NSC director for Asia. The primary American voice on DPRK policy for two decades.

DateCha's PositionOutcome
Dec 2011 "North Korea as we know it is over. Whether it comes apart in the next few weeks or over several months…" (NYT op-ed, "China's Newest Province?" Dec 19, 2011, after Kim Jong-il's death) Wrong KJU consolidated faster than KJI did.
2012 The Impossible State: markets eroding party control from below; contradictions unsustainable. Wrong KJU absorbed markets rather than being undone by them.
May/Jun 2026 "Cold peace": accept nuclear status de facto; focus on deterrence, not transformation. (Foreign Affairs) Pivot No correction offered. Cumings' 2002 position, rebranded.
The "cold peace" framework concedes what Cumings argued from the start: DPRK will not collapse, will not denuclearize under pressure, must be managed not transformed. Carnegie Endowment (2025) puts it directly: "It is now clear that applying pressure does not lead to North Korean restraint; rather, it fuels North Korean provocations."

The Korea Policy Institute frames the nuclear program as a rational response to an unresolved war: North Korea has been under military threat and economic embargo since 1950, never received the peace treaty the 1953 armistice called for, and watched the U.S. maintain tens of thousands of troops on its border. Under those conditions, the deterrent is not irrational — it is the predictable outcome of a security environment the U.S. created and maintains.

The Biggest Story: Yoon Tried to Start a War. DPRK Declined.

October–December 2024: The Drone Provocation

Source: Korea Times, April 24, 2026; UPI, April 24, 2026. Special prosecutors seeking 30-year sentence for insurrection.

Oct 3, 2024 ROK military drones fly over Pyongyang — ordered by Yoon Suk-yeol. Purpose per prosecutors: provoke DPRK military response to justify martial law declaration. Korea Times, Apr 24, 2026
Oct 9, 10, 2024 Two more drone flights over Pyongyang. North Korea does not retaliate militarily. UPI, Apr 24, 2026
Dec 3, 2024 Yoon declares martial law. National Assembly overturns it within hours.
Jan 15, 2025 Yoon arrested. Charged with insurrection. Later sentenced to life imprisonment in February. Korea Times
The analytical point: The standard framework treats DPRK as the irrational provocateur. Here, the ROK president ordered drone incursions over a sovereign capital — Pyongyang — to bait North Korea into attacking South Korea, as a domestic political maneuver. North Korea showed strategic restraint. A state that declines to be baited into war is not the irrational actor. The Korea Policy Institute has long argued that the source of instability on the peninsula is not Pyongyang's behavior but the unresolved war structure the U.S. and ROK maintain. October 2024 is Exhibit A.

Russia Changes the Economic Calculus

Arms for Lifeline: 2023–Present

Sept 13, 2023 KJU-Putin summit, Vostochny Cosmodrome. Arms transfer framework established. 38 North: "End to North Korea's Strategic Solitude?"
2023–2024 DPRK ships est. 1–3 million artillery shells (152mm, 122mm) plus short-range ballistic missiles to Russia for use in Ukraine. U.S. DoD; South Korean MND; Arms Control Association, May 2024
Mar 28, 2024 Russia vetoes renewal of UN Panel of Experts on DPRK Sanctions. Sanctions monitoring effectively ends. Russian ambassador: the panel had become a tool to "strangle North Korea." UN News, Apr 2024; Arms Control Association
Russia's UN ambassador framed the veto as ending a sanctions mechanism designed to "strangle" a sovereign state. DPRK's UN representative Kim Song made the same point: nuclear-armed states (the U.S., Russia, China, France, UK) face no comparable sanctions while demanding that DPRK disarm unilaterally. The UN debate transcript records both arguments in full. The economic strangulation model for forcing collapse now has no independent monitor and an active patron absorbing the cost.

KJU's Own Admission: 20×10

The 20×10 Project

Announced at the Tenth Session of the 14th Supreme People's Assembly, January 2024. Goal: build or renovate industrial facilities in 20 cities per year for ten years. KJU's stated objective: "eliminate the century-old backwardness of regions." (38 North, Feb 2025)

Year 1 Result 20 factories opened across North Korea, producing clothing, food, and household goods. Every province included. Williams & Ragnone, 38 North, Feb 27, 2025
Year 2+ Expansion beyond factories: hospitals, leisure centers, aquaculture facilities. KJU warned officials that factory failure due to raw material shortages "would constitute a crime before the Party and the state." 38 North; Eastern Angle
Cumings (p. 444) noted that market change was "trickling up from below." The 20×10 Project is the state-directed response: absorb and channel bottom-up pressure into party-led development, rather than allow markets to develop independently. Eastern Angle frames this as DPRK's "socialist reconstruction" succeeding where Western predictions of market-driven liberalization failed.

The Fourth Generation: Kim Ju-ae

Succession Timeline

Nov 18, 2022 Kim Ju-ae appears at Hwasong-17 ICBM launch with KJU. KCNA photographs her holding his hand. Analysts immediately flag: leaders do not bring children to missile tests as casual family outings. 38 North, Apr 2026
2022–2025 600+ appearances on state television. Military reviews, parade inspections, official ceremonies. Positioned to KJU's left — traditionally reserved for senior figures. Satake, 38 North, Apr 6, 2026
Feb 2026 South Korea's NIS formally assesses Kim Ju-ae as likely being groomed for succession. NIS director: "(She) can be seen as the successor… based on credible intelligence." The Diplomat, Feb 2026
Apr 2026 NIS reaffirms: Ju-ae has entered "formal designation stage." Internal DPRK skepticism noted: some officials doubt that a female leader can command loyalty in a patriarchal system. 38 North, Apr 2026
Recall Cumings (p. 429): a Soviet diplomat told him in the 1980s, "Come back in 2020 and see Jong Il's son inherit power." The dynasty continues. A fourth Kim generation in a nominally communist state has no parallel in communist history — and neither standard communist party theory nor Western liberal democratization theory predicted it. Cumings' framework, rooted in Korean dynastic political culture and anticolonial legitimacy, has more room for it than either.
한국어 자료 — Key Terms
주체
Chuch'e (Juche)
Self-reliance. DPRK's state ideology: political and ideological independence, not economic autarky. Formalized by Kim Il-sung in the 1970s.
선군
Sŏn'gun
Military-first. Kim Jong-il's response to famine and lost Soviet aid: elevate the KPA above the party. Cumings (p. 446): "an anomaly for a communist regime, and a tragedy for the people."
수령
Suryŏng
Supreme leader. Fuses state, party, and personal authority into a single body. The title has passed through three Kims; a fourth generation is now in discussion.
고난의 행군
Konanŭi haenggun
Arduous March — official term for the 1994–1998 famine. Est. 500K–1M deaths. The phrase invokes Kim Il-sung's anti-Japanese guerrilla campaign, framing starvation as heroic endurance.
핵 억제력
Haek ŏkcheryŏk
Nuclear deterrence. KJU declared it complete in September 2022 and wrote it into law — not a bargaining chip but a permanent security posture.
냉평화
Naeng p'yŏnghwa
Cold peace. Victor Cha's 2026 framework — de facto acceptance of nuclear status, deterrence over transformation. Cumings' position from 2002, arrived at twenty years late.

2024년 10월의 드론 사태는 "비합리적 행위자" 프레임의 한계를 극명하게 보여준다. 윤석열 대통령이 평양 상공에 드론을 보내 북한의 군사적 대응을 유도하려 한 반면, 북한은 전략적 자제를 선택했다. 코리아 폴리시 인스티튜트와 같은 비판적 학자들은 오래전부터 이 점을 지적해 왔다. 한반도 불안정의 근본 원인은 평양의 행동이 아니라, 종전 조약 없이 지속되는 전쟁 구조와 미국의 군사적 존재에 있다는 것이다.

Cumings: Still Right

Anticolonial legitimacy
The Kim family's anti-Japanese resistance credentials generate real political legitimacy. Korea Policy Institute frames this as the reason DPRK cannot be understood through a lens calibrated to Soviet-style communism.
Wrong comparison
East Germany collapsed; Cuba, Vietnam, and Iran did not. Regimes with genuine nationalist legitimacy behave differently than satellite states. Carnegie Endowment (2025) now accepts this implicitly.
Markets ≠ liberalism
Cumings (p. 444): markets were spreading from below. They did. KJU absorbed them. The liberalization thesis assumed party control and market growth were incompatible. They aren't.

Key Concepts

주체 / Juche
Self-reliance. Political independence from all outside powers. The ideological claim behind the nuclear program: DPRK answers to no one.
선군 / Sŏn'gun
Military-first. Cumings (p. 446): "an anomaly for a communist regime, and a tragedy for the people." KJU has modified but not abandoned it.
20×10 Project
KJU's 2024 industrial drive. "Century-old backwardness" — his phrase. A public admission that Juche-era development fell short.
Cold Peace
Cha's 2026 pivot: DPRK is here to stay. Cumings' position from 2002, without acknowledgment that it was Cumings' position from 2002.

Discussion Questions

1. The Yoon drone story inverts the standard "irrational DPRK" frame. Does Cumings' framework predict this event better than Cha's? What would each analyst say about it?
2. KJU declared the nuclear deterrent irreversible by law. The Carnegie "stable coexistence" paper accepts this. Is that a policy correction or a capitulation?
3. Kim Ju-ae: is this evidence of feudalism, or of dynastic political culture with Korean historical roots? What rides on that distinction analytically?
4. Russia's ambassador called the sanctions panel a tool to "strangle" a sovereign state. DPRK's UN representative called it a double standard. Are they wrong?
5. After reading this update, watch The Flower Girl (1972) as DPRK produced it. What does the film's nationalist framing look like through Cumings' lens vs. Cha's?
