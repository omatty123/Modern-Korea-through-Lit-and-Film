Classical Retreat

Impossible Withdrawal

The opening and rural-retreat sections are saturated with older literati models of withdrawal. The story raises them in order to test them, then shows why they no longer work under late colonial and postliberation conditions.

요순(堯舜) 때 농촌 / 도원경(桃源境)

Yao-Shun Rural Ideal and Peach Blossom Utopia

Text Glimpse
"요순(堯舜) 때 농촌" / "도원경(桃源境)"
The references appear in the opening argument about fleeing Seoul for a place beyond officials and wartime pressure.

Hyŏn imagines a village outside state violence, taxation, and wartime mobilization. The paired references invoke a sage-king past and a classical hidden-utopia tradition.

The point is not that such a place exists. The point is that Hyŏn wants it to exist, and the story immediately makes clear that it does not.

Where in text:Opening family debate about fleeing Seoul.
Why it matters:Sets up retreat as a fantasy the story will keep dismantling.
대산강진 / 한퇴지 / 도연명 / 팽택령

Taesan, Han Yu, and Tao Yuanming

Text Glimpse
"대산강진(對山姜瑨)의 비" / "한퇴지(韓退之)" / "도연명(陶淵明)"
This cluster comes when Hyŏn visits the old magistrate stelae and reads Taesan's poetry in the mountain town.

The magistrate-stela passage gathers several old models at once: Taesan Kang Jin as poet-magistrate, Han Yu as the literatus serving amid mountain scenery, and Tao Yuanming as the classic emblem of withdrawal from office.

Hyŏn admires the beauty of that world, but the passage turns into self-critique. He recognizes that refined mountain poetics and modern literary responsibility are no longer the same thing.

Where in text:Middle rural-retreat section, after Hyŏn visits the old magistrate stelae.
Why it matters:Transforms a local monument into an argument about what literature can no longer be.
두문동(杜門洞)

Dumundong and Loyalist Seclusion

Text Glimpse
"두문동(杜門洞)" / "그 두문동 구석으로나"
The place first appears in the Taesan landscape list, then returns in Kim Chikwŏn's farewell as a fantasy of retreat.

Dumundong evokes the tradition of loyalist withdrawal after dynastic change. In the rural section it is part of the old landscape of hiding and remembrance; later Kim Chikwŏn wants to retreat there in spirit.

The allusion lets the story connect private withdrawal with political refusal, then ask whether either still has public value.

Where in text:First in the Taesan passage, then again in Kim Chikwŏn's farewell.
Why it matters:Links local landscape memory to post-1945 conservative retreat.
춘향전 한 구절

Ch'unhyang as Vernacular Persistence

Text Glimpse
"춘향전 한 구절만 읽은 것"
The reference appears in the coercion sequence explaining why Hyŏn has already drawn military suspicion.

Hyŏn's trouble begins in part because, at a wartime lecture, he reads only a passage from Ch'unhyang in Korean. The gesture is small, but it matters.

The reference marks attachment to Korean literary speech at the very moment the colonial state is pressuring writers toward Japanese-language performance and visible collaboration.

Where in text:Early coercion section, before the war-history translation assignment.
Why it matters:Shows how even a minimal Korean textual gesture could register as political.
War Horizon

Global References Inside Korean Consciousness

The story is never just local. Hyŏn's inner life is repeatedly interrupted by European and Pacific war references, which is one reason the narrative feels so historically charged.

모로토프 / 히틀러 / 독소중립조약

The Molotov-Hitler Pact

Text Glimpse
"모로토프가 히틀러와 악수를 하고"
This lands inside Hyŏn's panicked self-questioning after he accepts the forced war-translation job.

Hyŏn recalls a German poet who despaired after Molotov and Hitler shook hands and the Soviet-German pact was signed. This is one of the story's clearest reminders that colonial Korean thought is not sealed off from European catastrophe.

The reference sharpens a moral question: what happens when the political force you trusted appears to betray history itself?

Where in text:Interior breakdown after Hyŏn accepts the war translation assignment.
Why it matters:Turns a Korean crisis of compromise into part of a larger anti-fascist crisis of faith.
연합군 / 정의와 역사의 법칙

The Allied War as Moral Horizon

Text Glimpse
"연합군의 승리" / "정의와 역사의 법칙"
Hyŏn answers the Molotov-Hitler shock by talking himself back toward historical faith.

Hyŏn answers despair by invoking the Allied war effort and the idea that history still has a lawful direction. This is not a decorative wartime reference; it is the structure of his temporary self-rescue.

The story keeps showing that politics enters consciousness as argument, not just event.

Where in text:Same interior passage as the Molotov-Hitler reference.
Why it matters:Frames liberation as part of a world-historical conflict rather than a purely national episode.
라바울

Rabaul and the Pacific War Clock

Text Glimpse
"일본군이 아직 라바울을 지킨다"
The place appears in Hyŏn's calculation of how many years the war may continue before Japan collapses.

Rabaul enters as a practical wartime indicator. Hyŏn calculates how long Japan might continue to hold out, and that estimate helps drive the decision to leave Seoul and wait in the countryside.

Even the move to the village is governed by global war geography.

Where in text:Beginning of the move to the mountain town.
Why it matters:Shows that the rural retreat is a strategic pause inside a global war timetable.
Dynastic and Postliberation History

Historical Analogy as Political Argument

In the Kim Chikwŏn debates, the story stops merely narrating and starts thinking through Korean history out loud. These allusions are not decorative scholarship. They are the terms in which Hyŏn justifies a politics.

광해군(光海君)

Kwanghaegun and Welfare over Empty Legitimism

Text Glimpse
"광해군(光海君) 아닙니까?"
The allusion appears mid-argument when Hyŏn defends a politics of protecting people over ritualized fidelity.

Hyŏn invokes Kwanghaegun to argue that protecting people's lives can matter more than preserving a pure line of loyalty. In the story's logic, this becomes a way to criticize rigid postliberation legitimism.

It is one of the most explicit moments where historical analogy becomes present-tense political reasoning.

Where in text:Hyŏn's extended debate with Kim Chikwŏn.
Why it matters:Recasts liberation politics through a familiar Korean dispute over principle and survival.
명(明) / 청태조(淸太祖)

Ming Loyalty and the Qing Challenge

Text Glimpse
"명(明)의 구원군" / "청 태조(淸太祖)"
These names appear in the same speech as Kwanghaegun, setting up the older diplomatic crisis Hyŏn wants readers to remember.

The Kwanghaegun argument is inseparable from the Ming-Qing historical frame behind it. Hyŏn uses that older diplomatic crisis to think about what happens when a weaker Korea is pulled into the claims of stronger powers.

The analogy lets him criticize pure moral posturing when the likely result is public disaster.

Where in text:Same debate sequence with Kim Chikwŏn.
Why it matters:Explains why the story treats political realism as ethically serious rather than merely cynical.
아관파천 / 대한

Agwan Pacheon and Great Han Nostalgia

Text Glimpse
"아관파천(俄館播遷)식" / "<대한><대한>"
This comes in Hyŏn's warning against symbolic independence without real guarantees or political preparation.

Hyŏn warns that if Korea receives only a nominal independence without strong guarantees, it may fall back into the kind of foreign-dependent intrigue associated with the late Yi court and the Russian legation crisis.

His target is not only old monarchy but also sentimental invocations of Great Han that ignore present geopolitical weakness.

Where in text:Later phase of the Kim Chikwŏn debate on trusteeship and independence.
Why it matters:Turns dynastic nostalgia into an object of critique rather than reverence.
삼팔선 / 한양성

The Thirty-Eighth Parallel and Hanyang

Text Glimpse
"<대한>을 찾아 삼팔선을 모험해 한양성(漢陽城)에"
The line appears in the final rooftop meditation as Kim Chikwŏn is re-read through larger historical patterns.

By the ending, the old capital and the new division line sit in the same sentence. That compression is deliberate: the story makes inherited political memory collide with the newly hardening geography of partition.

The allusion is not ancient, but it functions like one. It converts present politics into fate-laden symbolic geography.

Where in text:Final rooftop meditation on Kim Chikwŏn.
Why it matters:Shows liberation already collapsing into division inside the story's final image.
Terminal Analogy

Wang Guowei at the End

The final allusion is the most charged one in the story. Hyŏn cannot simply describe Kim Chikwŏn. He has to read him through another figure of dynastic loyalty and tragic historical belatedness.

왕국유(王國維) / 혼명호(混明湖)

Wang Guowei and Kunming Lake

Text Glimpse
"왕국유(王國維)" / "혼명호(混明湖)에 빠져"
These names enter as soon as Hyŏn sees Kim Chikwŏn walking away and begins recasting him as a figure of dynastic loyalty.

Wang Guowei becomes Hyŏn's final interpretive frame for Kim Chikwŏn: a cultivated man marked by loyalty to a dead order, dignified yet historically stranded. The story remembers both Wang's hairstyle and his death in Kunming Lake.

This is not casual comparison. It is the emotional key to Hyŏn's simultaneous sympathy for Kim and rejection of Kim's politics.

Who was Wang Guowei?
Where in text:Final rooftop scene after Kim Chikwŏn leaves Seoul.
Why it matters:Transforms a contemporary Korean figure into a cross-East-Asian allegory of noble but unusable loyalty.
상해 / 북경

Shanghai and Beijing as Afterlife Geography

Text Glimpse
"상해로 갔다가 북경으로"
The phrase appears inside the Wang Guowei memory, tracing the restless geography of a loyalist intellectual after dynastic collapse.

The Wang Guowei remembrance briefly opens outward into Shanghai and Beijing. These cities matter less as travel points than as stations in the fading trajectory of an ex-loyalist intellectual.

The ending therefore leaves Korea without really leaving it: foreign geography becomes the mirror in which Korean historical feeling is read.

Where in text:Same final passage on Wang Guowei.
Why it matters:Expands the story's ending into a regional meditation on revolution and loyalty.
