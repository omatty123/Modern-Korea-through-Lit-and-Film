Why DPRK Fiction?

North Korean literature is almost never taught in South Korean or Western classrooms. This is itself a political fact. The exclusion of DPRK writing from the Korean literary canon mirrors the political division of the peninsula — and perpetuates it. What we call "Korean literature" is, in practice, almost entirely the literature of one half of a divided nation.

The course's decision to include Han Ung-bin's stories alongside Han Sorya's Jackals (Week 3) is a deliberate corrective: to treat the peninsula's literary production whole, even across the divide. Reading DPRK fiction as literature — rather than as propaganda to be dismissed or evidence to be decoded — requires the same close reading skills applied to any other text.

DPRK Fiction in the Late 1990s

The late 1990s were an extraordinary moment in North Korea. The Arduous March (고난의 행군) — the famine of 1994–1998 — killed an estimated 240,000 to 3.5 million people (estimates vary widely). The state's capacity to provide for its citizens collapsed. Kim Il-sung had died in 1994; Kim Jong-il consolidated power through the crisis.

DPRK fiction from this period reflects a society under extreme stress, even if it cannot describe that stress directly. The official literary framework remained socialist realism — stories were expected to affirm collective values, loyalty to the party, and revolutionary spirit. But within those constraints, writers found ways to write about human relationships, everyday life, love, and loss.

Scholar Stephen Epstein, whose article "Encountering North Korean Fiction: The Origins of the Future" is assigned alongside these stories, argues that even highly constrained DPRK fiction rewards close reading — that the conventions themselves, and what they allow and forbid, are historically meaningful.

Reading DPRK Fiction: A Method

Approaching North Korean literature requires holding two things simultaneously: taking the text seriously as literature (craft, character, emotional truth) while also reading the constraints under which it was produced. Neither pure aesthetic reading (ignoring the political framework) nor pure ideological decoding (reducing the story to propaganda) is adequate.

Questions worth asking of any DPRK text: What human experiences does the story depict that would be recognizable across any society? Where does it strain against its own constraints — what does it approach obliquely, or what does it conspicuously not say? What values does it affirm, and how? And: what would change about this story if it had been written in Seoul instead of Pyongyang?

Placing Han Ung-bin's stories alongside Loyal Citizens of Pyongyang (the documentary also assigned in Week 9) is deliberate. The film presents North Korean migrants' testimonies about life in the DPRK; the fiction presents life in the DPRK from inside its own literary imagination. These are not the same thing, and the gap between them is instructive.

Key Concepts

Socialist Realism (사회주의 사실주의)
The official literary doctrine of the DPRK: fiction must depict reality in its "revolutionary development," affirm collective socialist values, and feature positive heroes. Individual interiority is subordinate to social purpose.
Arduous March (고난의 행군)
The North Korean famine of 1994–1998, caused by a combination of floods, drought, the collapse of Soviet aid, and regime mismanagement. Hundreds of thousands died; the social fabric of the DPRK was permanently altered.
Juche Literature (주체 문학)
DPRK literary theory grounding socialist realism in Kim Il-sung's Juche (self-reliance) ideology. Introduced as official doctrine in 1970s; governed what DPRK writers could and could not write.
Words Without Borders
Online literary magazine that has published some of the only English translations of contemporary DPRK fiction. Stephen Epstein's essay "Encountering North Korean Fiction" appears there.

Discussion Questions

1. What aspects of these stories feel universal — recognizable as human experience regardless of political system? What feels specific to the DPRK context?
2. Where do the stories strain against their own constraints — what do they approach obliquely, or conspicuously not address?
3. Compare Han Ung-bin's fiction with Han Sorya's Jackals (Week 3). Both are DPRK writers; what has changed in North Korean literary culture between 1951 and 1999?
4. The documentary Loyal Citizens of Pyongyang is also assigned this week. It presents testimony from North Koreans who left the DPRK. How does that testimony relate to the fictional world of these stories? Where do they confirm or contradict each other?
5. Epstein argues DPRK fiction is worth reading closely as literature, not just decoding as ideology. Do these stories support that claim?
